A B S T R A C T

Wormholes can effectively connect the fracture system as the dominant channels when carbonate matrix is acidified, thereby increasing the distance of acidification. It is critical to clarify the wormhole propagation law in carbonate reservoirs so as to optimize the acidification design. Previous studies highlight the influences of matrix as well as single simple fracture on wormhole propagation. However, the works characterizing impacts of single and multiple fractures with different geometry are insufficient. In this paper, the two-scale continuum model and pseudo-fracture model were combined to study the wormhole with different fractures. It was found that the fractures parallel to injection direction can concentrate fluids and thus accelerate the acid penetrating the formation, while the perpendicular ones will disperse fluids, thereby slowing down the breakthrough. The effect of straight fractures and arc fractures on wormholes are similar. When the inclination angle is less than 60∘ ,straight fracture and arc fracture can be regarded as parallel fracture of corresponding length. When the inclination angle is greater than 60∘ ,they can be regarded as superposition of parallel fracture and perpendicular fracture. As for the circular and polygonal fractures, only the parts near the outlet impact the subsequent wormhole propagation trajectories. The analysis of flow field shows that there is a control domain of about 3⋅L⋅cos⁡(θ) around the fractures. When there is no intersection of control domains of multiple fractures, there is no interference between them. There is no need to consider all fractures in the formation. Based on this, the step-by-step calculation method proposed in this paper is suitable for acidizing large-scale formation with complex fracture network.

1. Introduction

Carbonate reservoirs are rich in oil and gas resources. In order to improve the permeability of the formation near the wellbore, hydrochloric acid is often used for acidizing treatment. However, the intense reaction between carbonates and hydrochloric acid as well as the strong heterogeneity of the reservoir commonly contribute to the selective dissolution of large pores and thus the emergence of wormholes (Maheshwari et al., 2013; Ratnakar et al., 2013). Such wormholes, as the dominant conduits, can effectively connect the fracture system and increase the acidification distance. Therefore, it is of great significance to study the propagation law of wormholes in carbonate reservoirs so as to optimize the acidification design.
In the past decades, comprehensive works have been done to characterize wormholes. Hoefner and Fogler (1988) analyzed the morphology of wormholes through injection of hydrochloric acid into cores, after which numerous mathematical models have been established. In contrast to the capillary model (Daccord, 1993; Fredd and Fogler, 1998) and the semi-empirical model (Buijse and Glasbergen, 2005), Panga et al. (2005) built the two-scale continuum model that considers the convection, diffusion and acid-rock reaction. It succeeded to simulate the complex geometry of wormholes under different conditions and has been widely used. In order to determine the relationship between linear flow and radial flow wormhole fractal dimension, optimal acid injection rate, and minimum breakthrough pore volume, Kalia and Balakotaiah (2007) extended the two-scale continuum model to a polar coordinate system. Subsequently, works were done to characterize the factors that influence the wormhole propagation, including reservoir boundary geometry, compression region of the wormhole front, porosity distribution, and temperature (Cohen et al., 2008; Samadi et al., 2012; Maheshwari et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2017a). Most of these studies highlighted the effect of matrix on the propagation of wormholes. There were only a few studies on the effects of widely distributed fractures in the carbonate reservoirs, which mainly paid attention to the single simple fracture (Szymczak and Anthony, 2012). However, fracture geometry could be highly complex due to the nonuniform distribution of ground stress in space and the long-term geological transformation of carbonate reservoirs in the burial process. It requires specific attention to investigate the effects of the single and multiple fractures with different geometry on the propagation of wormholes.

In the reservoir, the fracture refers to the region with a porosity of nearly 1 , whose width is small compared to the length and height. Generally, along the direction of length, the pressure gradient of acid is large and the flow velocity is fast. It is the main propagation direction of the wormhole. Along the direction of height, the pressure gradient of acid is small, and it has little effect on the wormhole. Two dimensional and three dimensional models are often used to study the growth process of wormholes. Compared with the three dimensional model, the two dimensional model not only considers the relationship between the fracture and the injection direction of the acid, but also reduces the interference of the third dimension and greatly reduces the calculation of the model. And it can simulate the effect of many kinds of fractures in the reservoir on the propagation of the wormholes. Based on the two dimensional two-scale continuum model, the fracture in this paper is regarded as the matrix with certain porosity. By comparing Darcy formula with the laminar flow formula, the value of porosity is determined to be 0.999 . In this way, the influence of fracture geometry on wormhole propagation can be studied. Note that all the mathematical formulas in this paper are solved by finite element software, Comsol Multiphysics.

2. Mathematical model

2.1. Acid flow in the matrix

When the acid is injected into the formation at a certain rate, there is a mass transfer of hydrogen ions from the pore fluid to the carbonate rock surface under the action of macroscopic motion (convection) and concentration gradient (diffusion). In this mass transfer process, the formation porosity is altered. To simulate this process, the paper made the following assumptions: (1) the formation is saturated with fluid, and the fluid is incompressible; (2) the viscosity of the pore fluid is close to that of the acid, the influences of gravity and capillary forces are ignorable.

The former case needs to consider the consumption of hydrogen ions on the rock surface and the change of porosity (Eq. (3)), while the latter does not (Eq. (4)).

2.2. Acid flow in the fracture

The flow of acid in the fracture is different from that in the matrix. The former is free flow controlled by the Navier-Stokes equation, while the latter is the porous media seepage controlled by Darcy's equation. Because the geometry of fractures will change constantly after contacting acid, it is very complicated to couple the Navier-Stokes equation with Darcy equation.
Some scholars have proposed different models to the problems of free flow and porous media seepage. They include the fracture network model (Dong et al., 2002), pseudo-fracture model (Li et al., 2016; Kalia and Balakotaiah, 2007), discrete-fracture model (Liu et al., 2017b), and so on. The fracture network model assumes that acid flows only along the fracture, and the leakage coefficient is used to describe the flow of acid in the matrix. It cannot be used to describe complicated fracture systems near wellbore. The pseudo-fracture model regards the fracture as an area with high porosity and treats the fracture and matrix in the same way. The method is simple in concept and can effectively simulate the dissolution process of in fractured formation. However, it has high requirements for computer memory and operation ability. The discrete-fracture model deals with the fracture and matrix in different ways. It assumes that the fracture aperture remains unchanged, the acid will only increase the porosity of the adjacent matrix. After a series of transformations, the equation is solved. This method has high computational efficiency, but the processing method is rather complicated and requires special skills.
It was noticed that although the classical two-scale continuum model only studied the wormholes in the matrix (Panga et al., 2005), as the regions with porosity close to 1 , the role of wormholes in the matrix was almost the same as that of fractures. The two-scale continuum model regards the wormhole as an area of considerable permeability and uses Darcy's law to describe the flow of fluid in the wormhole, which means that fractures can also be treated in this way. Conversely, if the fluid in the fracture must be considered strictly in accordance with free flow, so should the fluid in the wormhole. This dynamic coupling is obviously quite difficult. Therefore, the pseudo-fracture model is adopted in the paper, and the fracture is regarded as a region with considerable permeability. In order to speed up the calculation, the fracture area which has a great influence on the pressure field is subdivided intensively, and the matrix is subdivided by coarse mesh. 3. Results and analysis

3.1. The effect of single fracture geometry on the propagation of wormhole

Previous physical simulation experiments and numerical simulation studies suggest the sequential emergence of face dissolution, wormhole dissolution, and uniform dissolution patterns with the increase of flow velocity (Kalia and Balakotaiah, 2009; Liu et al., 2017a; Ma et al., 2017). With the model parameters in this paper, when the flow velocity is 0.05cm/s ,the breakthrough pore volume is the smallest. However, there are a large number of fractures in the actual reservoir, and their location, length, width, shape, and number may affect the dissolution results. The effect of single fracture geometry on the wormhole propagation is investigated and elaborated below.

3.1.1. Straight fractures

Tectonic stress is the main cause of fractures. When the direction of ground stress coincides with that of the fracture, the ground stress has a secondary transformation effect on natural fractures, which is conducive to maintaining the open state and efficient diversion capability of natural fractures. Conversely, the natural fractures will be suppressed by the ground stress, which reduces the conductivity. The fractures in carbonate reservoirs are mostly modified by the maximum horizontal principal stress, and most of them are parallel fractures. However, due to the complexity of the ground stress distribution, there are a large number of high-angle and vertical fractures in carbonate reservoirs. It is necessary to study the influence of fracture inclination on the propagation of wormholes.

As can be seen from Fig. 5,when the fracture inclination is 0∘ (parallel to the injection direction), the main wormhole propagates along the direction of the fracture at the inlet, and those wormholes in the other directions are short, which suggest a strong attracting effect on the acid by the fracture parallel to the injection direction. As the inclination increases, wormholes in the other directions also well propagate, and the main wormhole along the fracture direction tends to branch off. In the extreme case, where the fracture is perpendicular to the injection direction, it can be observed that the wormholes in the other directions greatly propagate at the inlet, while the main wormhole branches off at the outlet. It indicates that the fracture perpendicular to the acid injection direction disperses the acid, forming multiple channels.
In order to further describe the effect of fracture inclination on the propagation of wormholes, the breakthrough pore volume curves corresponding to different inclinations are plotted. It can be compared with the breakthrough pore volume of the fracture-free formation so as to calculate the degree of change, δ ,which is influenced by fracture inclinations.

Fig. 6 shows that the breakthrough pore volume curve is approximately symmetrical about θ=0∘ ,where the breakthrough pore volume reaches the smallest. It is 29.16% smaller than the breakthrough pore volume of the fracture-free formation. As the absolute value of the inclination increases, the breakthrough pore volume increases, and the accelerating effect of fractures on the acid penetrating the formation weakens. In the extreme case (θ=±90∘) ,instead of accelerating the breakthrough,the fractures slow down it. All these are related to the development mechanism of wormholes, which is mainly attributed to the uneven dissolution of the rock by acid. As for the uneven dissolution, it is mainly related to the pressure field and velocity field of the heterogeneous formation. Parallel to the acid injection direction, the pressure gradient is large and the flow velocity is fast, which are favorable for wormhole propagation. Meanwhile, perpendicular to the acid injection direction, the pressure gradient is small and the flow velocity is slow, which mainly determine the diameter of the wormhole. Therefore, slightly inclined straight fractures can contribute to a more concentrated velocity field, inducing and accelerating the propagation of wormholes at the inlet (as shown in Fig. 7), while largely inclined straight fractures disperse the acid, which hinders the wormhole propagation and thus slows down the breakthrough (as shown in Fig. 8).

As can be observed in Fig. 6, the variation of breakthrough pore volume with fracture inclination seems to coincide with the cosine curve. Therefore, this study assumes that fractures of different inclinations can be decomposed into two components. One is parallel to the acid injection direction with the length of L⋅cos⁡(θ) , while the other is perpendicular to the acid injection direction with the length of L⋅sin⁡(θ) . The former one can concentrate fluids to accelerate the breakthrough, while the latter one disperses fluids, thus slowing down the breakthrough. These two components commonly determine the actual impact of fractures on the penetration of acid through the formation.
To verify the above hypothesis, we artificially make a parallel fracture with the length of L⋅cos⁡(θ) and a perpendicular fracture with the length of L⋅sin⁡(θ) at the same locations. Then their influences on the degree of change are respectively compared with that of corresponding straight fractures, as shown in Figs. 12 and 13.
It can be seen from Fig. 9 that the curves of parallel and straight fractures are quite similar in the case of θ<60∘ ,in both of which δ has a good linear relationship with L⋅cos⁡(θ) . Fig. 11 also shows that wormholes emerge in all the parallel fractures with different lengths, and the wormhole diameter is negatively related to the fracture length. This means that when the absolute value of the fracture inclination is not very large, it is reasonable to consider the straight fractures as parallel fractures with length of L⋅cos⁡(θ) to determine the breakthrough pore volume.
The effect of perpendicular fractures on the propagation of wormholes is different from that of parallel fractures. Due to the dispersion of acid by the perpendicular fractures, wormhole propagation slows down before contacting the fracture, and the dissolved volume at the inlet is enhanced. However, after contacting the fracture, the flow path of the main wormhole suddenly expands, and consequently the acid flow disperses. As a result, the main wormhole generally braches off along the two ends and the middle of the fracture, which results in the emergence of multiple wormholes and thus increases the randomness of the wormhole propagation path. Therefore, it is difficult to clarify the complicated relationship between perpendicular fracture length L⋅sin⁡(θ) and δ ,which is also demonstrated in Figs. 10 and 12.

Fig. 10. Influences of perpendicular and straight fractures on degree of change.
It is found by plotting influences of parallel, perpendicular and straight fractures on degree of change (Fig. 13) that the straight fractures can be approximated as parallel fractures with the length of L⋅cos⁡(θ) when the inclination θ is less than 60∘ . However,when the inclination is large, the straight fractures have to be considered as the combination of the two kinds of fractures.

3.1.2. Arc fractures

Obviously, fractures in the formation cannot be entirely linear. They are actually often tortuous due to the non-uniform distribution of the ground stress, which can be quantitatively characterized by curvature. In order to simplify the model, this section assumes the fracture curvature to be a constant, while two tangent arcs with the central angle of 90∘ are used to represent the straight fracture. The degree of change with inclination are respectively plotted for the arc and straight fractures to identify whether they have consistent influence on wormhole propagation (see Fig. 14 and Table 3).
As can be seen from Fig. 15, the degree of change with arc fracture inclination is similar to that with straight fracture inclination, although the former one is more complex. The inclination corresponding to the minimum value in the arc fracture case is −30∘ , while that in the straight fracture case is 0∘ . In order to explain this difference, their porosity fields corresponding to inclinations of −30∘,−15∘ and 0∘ are respectively compared.
Fig. 16 shows that for the fractures with inclinations of −30∘ to 0∘ ,the wormhole always first connect the inlet end of the fracture and then follows the fracture. The fracture with inclination of 0∘ has the largest projection along the injection direction, and therefore it has the strongest acceleration effect on the breakthrough. For arc fractures, acid does not necessarily flow into the fracture from point A because the tangent direction of the fracture is not the same as the end-to-end line AB. Instead, the acid tends to flow into the fracture from a point where the acid injection direction is tangent to the fracture direction, which can be verified by the acid flow-out direction that is tangent to the fracture direction. Therefore, arc and straight fractures have different influences on the wormhole propagation.

3.1.3. Circular and polygonal fractures

Abundant long fractures in carbonate reservoirs often interact with each other to form fracture networks. Moreover, these fractures are generally influenced by tectonics and karstification (Wang et al., 2014). Therefore, there are some circular and polygonal fractures in the carbonate reservoirs. To facilitate the study, these fractures are first assumed to be in the form of well symmetrical circles, and their influence on wormhole propagation is investigated (see Fig. 17 and Table 4).
As can be seen from Figs. 18 and 19, the acid penetration into the formation can be divided into three stages: (1) before contacting the fracture, acid is attracted by the fracture; (2) after contacting the fracture, acid preferentially flows along the high-permeability channel, and the hydrogen ion concentration rises rapidly; (3) after the fracture is saturated with acid, the acid flows along the right circular arc of the fracture, forming radial wormholes. It can be deduced that the part near the inlet of the fracture is only the channel for acid, while geometry of the part near the outlet of the fracture has a great influence on the propagation trajectory of the subsequent wormholes.

3.2. The effect of multiple fractures geometry on the propagation of wormhole

In Section 3.1, the effect of single fracture geometry on the wormhole was discussed. However, there are many fractures with different shapes and sizes in carbonate reservoirs. As high-permeability channels, these fractures affect the growth of wormholes together. It is essential to study the influence of multiple fracture geometry on the propagation of wormhole. Among the factors affecting the wormhole propagation, the flow behavior of acid is undoubtedly the most important. The flow field can well show this flow behavior. Therefore, this section mainly uses the flow field to analyze the role of multiple fractures in the formation.

3.2.1. The control domain of fractures

In order to study the influence of multiple fractures on wormhole propagation, it is necessary to define the influence area of single fracture on fluid. For simplicity, we first observe the flow field around the parallel fracture.
The black lines in Fig. 23 are streamlines, and the density of the streamlines indicates the velocity of the flow. From the flow field, it can be seen that the streamlines inside the red wire frame bend obviously towards the fracture, while the streamlines outside are nearly parallel. This means that the acid in the red wire frame is attracted by the fracture, and the acid in other areas is hardly affected. Therefore, it can be considered that the actual influence area of the fracture on acid is the red wire frame area in Fig. 23. It is named as the control domain of fractures.

From Figs. 23-25, it can be found that although the length, inclination angle and shape of fractures in the actual formation are different, the influence of fractures on acid is local, and the size of control domain is proportional to L⋅cos⁡(θ) . 3.2.2. The interaction between fractures

In this section, the size of calculation domain is set to 30×30cm ,and the size of fracture is set to 3×0.2cm . The interaction between fractures is studied when the fracture is located outside and inside the control domain of other fracture, respectively.
(1) Fracture is located outside the control domain of other fracture
Section 3.2.1 shows that the control domain size of parallel fracture with a length of 3cm is 9cm . Therefore,three parallel fractures are set at different locations with spacing of more than 9cm to study whether there is interference between the fractures which are far apart. Fig. 27 shows the distribution of fractures. Figs. 28 and 29 show the porosity and flow fields.
Fig. 35. Porosity field at different time when there are multiple fractures in formation.
As shown in Fig. 28, three short wormholes are formed at the entrance. One of the wormholes connectes fracture A and continues to grow, but fails to connect fracture B and C. This can be explained by Fig. 29. Fig. 29(a) shows that there are some control domains near the three fractures. Because fracture A is closest to the entrance, wormhole first occurs and propagates along the fracture. Fracture B and C are far away from the fracture A ,and their control domain cannot cover the existing wormhole. Therefore, the subsequent growth trajectory of wormholes is hardly affected by these two fractures. In order to verify this rule, only fracture A is retained, and it is found that the growth trajectory of wormholes is basically unchanged (as shown in Fig. 30).

(2) Fracture is located inside the control domain of other fracture

The position of fracture B is moved towards fracture A to make it inside the control domain of fracture A ,and the inclination angle of fracture B also increases. Porosity and flow field are shown in Figs. 31 and 32.
The streamline distribution between fractures A and B in Fig. 32 (b) shows that when the two fractures are close to each other, the flow behavior of acid is affected by both control domains. After the wormhole connects fracture A ,it tends to grow towards fracture B . The growth path of wormhole is affected by the geometry of fracture B near the distance,but has little relation with fracture C whose control area is far away. This can be verified from Figs. 31 and 33.

3.2.3. The effect of multiple fractures geometry on the wormhole and its application

Firstly, the fractures with different shapes, lengths, inclination angles and spacing are set in the calculation domain of 50×50cm ,and the acidification process of fractured formation is simulated. Fig. 34 shows the distribution of fractures.
Figs. 35 and 36 show that all the fractures in the calculation domain form local control domains. The control domains of fractures A and B are closest to the entrance,and acid enters these two fractures preferentially to form wormholes. Subsequently, fractures C,D and E closer to the wormhole become the channels in turn. The flow field of Fig. 36(c) shows that there are concentrated streamlines near the fracture A, C, D and E. Acid flows directly into the formation in this direction, while the presence of fracture F and G has little effect on the growth of subsequent wormholes.
Fig. 36. Flow field at different time when there are multiple fractures in formation.
From the above acidification process, we can get the following understanding. (1) The formation position of initial wormhole is determined by the control domain closest to the entrance, but not by the fractures in the distant formation, unless the distant fractures are so long that their control domains cover the entrance. (2) Wormholes and fractures have similar control domains. Once a wormhole becomes the dominant channel, the growth of other wormholes inside the control domain of this wormhole almost ceases. And the subsequent growth of wormhole is determined by the control domain of surrounding fractures.
At present, carbonate reservoirs often contain complex fractures and require long acidizing distance. The increase of the size and complexity of the corresponding mathematical model will bring great challenges to the computer memory and the convergence of the model. Most of the acidification models are often small in size, and it is difficult to meet the actual acidification design requirements. From the previous analysis, it can be found that the fractures that determine the growth trajectory of wormholes are those whose control domains can cover the tip of wormholes. Therefore, there is no need to consider all fractures in the formation. A natural idea is that for large-scale models with complex fractures, the method of step-by-step calculation can be used to reduce the computation cost. 4. Conclusions

(1) The parallel fractures can concentrate fluids and accelerate the acid penetrating the formation, while the perpendicular ones will disperse fluids, thereby slowing down the breakthrough. The effect of straight fractures and arc fractures on wormholes are similar. When the inclination angle is less than 60∘ ,straight fracture and arc fracture can be regarded as parallel fracture of corresponding length. When the inclination angle is greater than 60∘ ,they can be regarded as superposition of parallel fracture and perpendicular fracture.
(2) As for the circular and polygonal fracture, only the part near the outlet of the fracture influences the propagation trajectory of subsequent wormholes, which tend to continue propagating at the inflection point. When the outlet of the fracture is polygonal, X-shaped wormholes form at both sides; when the outlet of the fracture is circular, radial wormholes propagate along the normal direction of the arc fracture.
(3) The analysis of flow field shows that the influence of fractures on acid is local, and there is a control domain of about 3.L.cos (θ) around the fractures. The acid inside the control domain flows centrally to the fracture, while the acid outside is almost unaffected. When there is no intersection of control domains of multiple fractures, there is no interference between them. When the control domains of multiple fractures intersect, the acid flowing through one fracture tends to approach another fracture.
(4) The fractures that determine the growth trajectory of wormholes are those whose control domains can cover the tip of wormholes. There is no need to consider all fractures in the formation. Based on this conclusion, the step-by-step calculation method proposed in this paper has the advantages of good simulation effect and less computation, and is suitable for acidizing large-scale formation with complex fracture network.
